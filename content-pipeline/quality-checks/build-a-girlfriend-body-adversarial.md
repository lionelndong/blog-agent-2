# Adversarial review — build-a-girlfriend-body

Read as a skeptical industry expert who has seen 100 AI-generated articles on AI companion / character creators. Brand: Pleasur.AI. Audience: adults searching "build a girlfriend body."

## Five weakest things

**1. The "seven decisions in order" framing is a bullet list pretending to be insight.**
H2 #2 lays out art style → archetype → ethnicity → hair → body type → sliders → outfit, and the article keeps gesturing at this as if it's a hard-won discovery. It isn't. It's the literal click order of every creator wizard in the category. Calling it "the difference between a build that lands in one pass and one that eats fifty regenerations" (line 9) is the kind of false-stakes hook that experienced readers see through immediately. The table at lines 55–63 mostly restates the bullets above it.

**2. The numbers in the SERP-gap section are unsupported and arithmetically suspicious.**
"Nine of the top ten Google results...are product landing pages or parasite listicles. The lone how-to ranker pulls four monthly visitors." Then "the traffic potential of the #1 ranking page is roughly 19,000 visits a month." Four versus 19,000 across the same SERP needs more than `[link]` to land. "Six of ten...don't disclose a concrete monthly price" / "Three of six advertise breast and butt sliders" / "Two of six advertise butt sliders" — the denominators shift between "ten," "six," and "six of ten" inside three paragraphs. Readers tracking the math will notice.

**3. H2 #6 stat-stuffs at the expense of the actual privacy argument.**
The Pew 63% figure, Statista's 30%, the EU AI Act, and the UK OSA all land in 11 lines (166–176). The argument the section is trying to make — "name what the platform stores: chat history, payment, image content" — gets one paragraph (170–172) sandwiched between data points that don't change the recommendation. The EU AI Act and UK OSA in particular ("None of this changes how you build a body") are explicitly admitted to be off-topic, then included anyway. This is the SEO move of citing for citation density, not for argument.

**4. The slider section solves a problem with hand-waving math.**
"Most realistic models produce believable output across roughly 70% of the range. The top and bottom 15% produce uncanny output." (lines 110–112) Where does 70% come from? "Stay inside one standard deviation of the body-type preset's center" (line 120) — what's the standard deviation of a slider with no exposed numerical range? The advice ("if you want high-volume, pick curvy or hourglass and leave the slider mid") is sound. The pseudo-quantitative dressing makes it sound like a research finding when it's a workflow tip.

**5. The closing thought in H2 #5 (lines 152–154) is a product-tease bolted onto a body-build article.**
"Coming this week — you tap the speaker icon..." The article was about creator-screen decisions; voice replies and phone calls have nothing to do with the body-build flow. They live in chat, after creation. Dropping them at the closing of the age/outfit section is brand promo wedged into the wrong H2. A reader who came for "build a girlfriend body" finishes that section thinking the article got distracted.

## One thing that genuinely works

The slider-conflict failure mode in H2 #4 (lines 114–118): "athletic" body type plus maxed breast slider, "petite" plus maxed butt slider, "the model resolves the conflict by producing something that looks edited rather than rendered." That's a specific, falsifiable observation a reader can check against their own regeneration history. It's the only place the article tells the reader something the SERP genuinely doesn't.
