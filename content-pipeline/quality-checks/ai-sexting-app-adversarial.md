# Adversarial review — ai-sexting-app

A skeptical industry expert reads this draft. 400 words on the weakest things, 1 thing that works.

## Five weakest things

1. **The "Best for:" tag at the end of each pick reads like a feature card, not editorial.** Three of the four picks end with `Best for: someone who wants...`. That's the same scaffolding every affiliate roundup uses. The piece opens claiming to be different from listicles — then closes each section with the listicle's signature move. Either drop the tag or vary its prose so it doesn't read as templated. Right now it's textbook AI-roundup furniture.

2. **The CrushOn pricing line is a hedge that telegraphs uncertainty.** "Pricing starts around $5.99/mo per third-party reviews — we couldn't verify exact tiers live in this run." A reader doesn't want to hear that you couldn't get the page to load. Either kill the price-anchor and say "see the deeper-dive review for current pricing," or come back with the live number. Hedging mid-pick undercuts trust everywhere else.

3. **"We'll tell you exactly where on the list" in the intro is a promise the article never cashes.** Pleasur.AI is #4. That's an ordering choice but the piece doesn't say so. It treats #4 as a neutral position. The reader who clicked because of "honest picks" expected something like "we put ourselves last on purpose, here's why." Without that beat, the disclosure is half-done.

4. **The privacy section name-checks Mozilla and HIBP without locating either claim in the specific apps under review.** The reader gets a category-level scare stat, then nothing telling them whether Candy or OurDream or CrushOn have the same exposure or not. The section reads as obligation, not analysis. If the privacy floor is "a real buying criterion," the piece needs at least one comparative line — e.g. "Of the four, only X publishes a vulnerability-disclosure process."

5. **The FAQ's first answer is a legal hedge.** "Yes, between consenting adults using a platform that enforces age verification. The legal pressure points are minors..." The reader who asked this wanted a one-word yes, not a paragraph about regulatory pressure. The FAQ format already signals "give me a quick answer." This one over-explains and reads like the brand's lawyer dictated half of it.

## One thing that genuinely works

The intro lead — "Most 'best AI sexting app' listicles name fourteen apps and recommend none of them. That's not editorial. That's an affiliate dashboard pretending to be advice." That's a real opinion stated cleanly with a specific number anchor, and it sets a tone the rest of the piece mostly keeps. The first three sentences alone do more editorial work than most competing articles do in 2,000 words.
