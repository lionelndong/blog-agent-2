## The six things that actually matter when evaluating a platform

**Target:** ~700 words (longest section — the meat of the guide)

- **BLUF:** Most platform comparisons focus on the wrong things. What actually predicts whether you'll still be using a platform a month from now is memory, character coherence, image quality, voice integration, friction, and uncensored honesty — in roughly that order.
- **Key points (each becomes a sub-thread of 80–120 words):**

  1. **Memory across long conversations** — Almost every platform claims memory. Few hold up past conversation 5. Test by referencing something specific you said three sessions ago and see if the character responds coherently.

  2. **Character coherence under pressure** — Does your custom character stay in character when the conversation gets weird, emotional, or contradictory? Generic models break down here; finely tuned ones don't.

  3. **Image quality and integration** — Are images locked to a separate page, or can the character send you a selfie mid-chat? In-conversation image generation is a massive UX win and rare.

  4. **Voice — not as a separate mode, but inside chat** — Most platforms treat voice as a binary: text mode or voice mode. The platforms that get this right let you tap a single message to hear it spoken — voice as a per-message action, not a separate app within the app. **[Pleasur.AI angle — this is exactly how Voice Replies will work when they ship this week]**

  5. **Friction to start a conversation** — Sign-ups, paywalls, character setup. The platforms that win make the first 60 seconds feel like opening a chat with someone you already know. The ones that lose make you fill out a form first.

  6. **Honest "uncensored" claims** — Every platform says "uncensored." Few are. Test it on day one with the scenarios YOU care about; assume any platform that hedges in the FAQ will hedge in the chat.

## How to create a companion that doesn't feel generic

**Target:** ~500 words

- **BLUF:** Most "AI girlfriend" experiences fail not because of the model, but because of a thin character setup — three personality sliders and a hairstyle don't make a person.
- **Key points:**
  - **Backstory matters more than appearance.** A character with a profession, a hometown, a few opinions, and one specific quirk feels alive after 10 minutes. A character with nothing but visual settings feels generic after 10 messages.
  - **Conversation style is its own dimension.** "Witty, sarcastic, soft-hearted, confident, melancholic" — pick 2–3 dominant traits and stick with them.
  - **Set what they like AND what annoys them.** Asymmetry creates personality. A character who genuinely doesn't like long-winded compliments feels real.
  - **Use the platform's voice profile thoughtfully** — voice carries personality more than a 200-word backstory does. If the platform has voice, audition a few before committing.
  - **Don't over-design the first time.** Make a rough character, chat for an hour, then iterate. Real personalities are revealed in conversation, not in setup forms.

## The honest limits

**Target:** ~400 words

- **BLUF:** Every AI girlfriend platform on the market today has the same four limits — memory decay, response repetition, factual unreliability, and a privacy footprint that's worth reading about — and pretending otherwise is the most common failure mode of category coverage.
- **Key points:**
  - **Memory decays.** Top-of-context conversations stay sharp; deep historical references drift. Best platforms paper over this with summary memory; none have solved it.
  - **Responses get repetitive.** Stylistic patterns emerge in any LLM after enough turns. The good platforms cycle phrasing more aggressively; none eliminate it.
  - **Don't trust factual claims.** AI companions are character-first, not knowledge-first. Asking yours about current events, medical advice, or legal questions is a category mistake.
  - **Read the privacy policy.** "End-to-end encrypted" is a marketing phrase that means different things on different platforms. Check what's logged, retained, and sharable.
