## How AI porn is actually made (plain English)

**Target:** ~450 words

- **BLUF:** Modern AI porn is a stack of three things — a diffusion model that turns noise into images, a fine-tune (LoRA) that biases that model toward a particular style or character, and a chat layer (a language model with a custom system prompt) that decides what the character says — and once you understand those three pieces, the entire category stops feeling like magic.
- **Key points:**
  - **Diffusion, in one paragraph:** the model starts with random static and iteratively "denoises" it into an image that matches a prompt. Stable Diffusion popularized this in 2022; the open-source weights are why the entire adult-AI category exists in the first place.
  - **LoRAs and fine-tunes** — small "personality adapters" trained on a specific look, character, or aesthetic. This is how character consistency works across multiple images of the same companion.
  - **The chat side** is a language model (Llama 3.x, Mistral, or a hosted model behind an API) wrapped in a system prompt that holds the character's backstory, voice, and conversation style. "Memory" is whatever fits in the context window plus a summary layer.
  - **Voice and video are the newer floors of the same building** — TTS voice cloning for replies, image-to-video models like Sora-class systems for short clips. Quality jumped between 2024 and 2026 enough that "AI porn videos" is now a 19,000/month search.

## What's legal, what's not, and what to know before you generate anything

**Target:** ~400 words

- **BLUF:** Generating fictional adult characters as an adult on yourself is legal in the US in 2026; generating non-consensual sexual images of a real person isn't, and the federal TAKE IT DOWN Act plus a growing patchwork of state deepfake laws makes the second category personally and financially expensive — quickly.
- **Key points:**
  - **TAKE IT DOWN Act (signed May 19, 2025)** mandates 48-hour removal of non-consensual intimate imagery (NCII) from platforms that receive a takedown notice; failure carries FTC enforcement.
  - **State deepfake laws** are stacking — Tennessee's ELVIS Act, California's AB 602, New York's deepfake statute. The Baltimore and Tennessee Grok suits are testing the same theory on platform liability.
  - **CSAM is a separate, much harder line.** The 2024 IWF report found roughly 3,500 AI-generated CSAM images on the dark web — every one of them prosecutable. No legitimate platform serves this; any platform that doesn't have an explicit refusal policy isn't legitimate.
  - **For the user, the practical floor is simple:** the work has to be of a fictional adult character, generated by you on yourself, on a platform that documents its training data and refuses real-person likeness. That's the safe-harbor zone; everything else is risk.
