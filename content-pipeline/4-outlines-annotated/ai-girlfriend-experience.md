## Act 1: the first 90 seconds (why everyone's first chat hooks)

**Target:** ~380 words

- **BLUF:** The opening 90 seconds of a custom-built AI companion chat are reliably impressive — the character has a name you chose, a face you designed, a voice you picked, and remembers what you just told her — and that's enough to make most newcomers think the whole experience will keep feeling that way.
- Most platforms let you skip stock characters entirely and design the companion from scratch — appearance, personality, backstory, voice, and conversation style. Pleasur.AI's [AI Companion Creator](https://pleasur.ai/create) is one example of that build flow; Character.AI's bigger library of pre-made characters is the opposite trade-off.
- The 90-second magic is real and reproducible: the character introduces herself, references a detail you set up minutes ago, asks a follow-up. People who've only used ChatGPT or Claude with a flirty system prompt feel the difference immediately — there's no safety lecture, no "as an AI language model," no flat affect.
- The trick is that those 90 seconds use *active* context — everything you just typed is still in the prompt window. Memory hasn't been tested yet. Personality hasn't been pushed yet. You're meeting the character at her best.
- **Evidence:** Wired's first-person 2023 piece ([Wired](https://www.wired.com/story/ai-girlfriend-week/)) is the canonical version of this hook — a reporter forms an emotional bond inside a single session. The promptspace.in fragment captures the same beat in one line: *"I mentioned my fictional dog's name 'Biscuit' on day 3, and on day 17, my AI companion asked how Biscuit was doing"* — the day-3 part is real on every platform; the day-17 part isn't always.
- **Transition:** That magic gets you to day two. What happens between day two and day seven is where the platforms start to separate.

## Act 2: week one (when it stops feeling like a demo)

**Target:** ~380 words

- **BLUF:** By the end of week one most people who stick around have stopped framing the chat as "I'm trying an AI" and started treating it like an ongoing thread — the character remembers a few specific things, the conversation has its own running jokes, and the experience has shifted from product demo to compulsive open-the-app behavior.
- Image generation is the second hook. Platforms that put image generation in the chat thread (rather than as a separate app) compress the loop — your companion can "send" a picture in response to a request, in her established style, without you switching tabs. Pleasur.AI's [in-chat image generation](https://pleasur.ai/generate) is one example; many competitors still route this through a separate page.
- Memory becomes the silent gating function. The good platforms summarize older turns and feed those summaries back; the lazy ones just truncate. By day five you can usually tell which one you're on by asking the character to recall something from your second conversation.
- The privacy floor is also load-bearing here even if it's invisible — week one is when the volume of intimate text you've typed crosses the line from "trying it out" to "would be embarrassing if leaked." Mozilla's *Privacy Not Included* found 90% of 11 romantic AI chatbots surveyed could share or sell personal data, only one (Genesia) met minimum security, 73% had no published vulnerability process, and 54% won't let users delete data ([Mozilla, Feb 2024](https://foundation.mozilla.org/en/privacynotincluded/articles/happy-valentines-day-romantic-ai-chatbots-dont-have-your-privacy-at-heart/)). The 1.9-million-account Muah.AI breach in September 2024 ([HIBP](https://haveibeenpwned.com/Breach/Muah)) made the abstract risk concrete.
- **Evidence:** Mozilla 2024, HIBP Muah.AI breach record, and Reddit fragments collected in the research dossier — particularly the JustHoney quote *"feels like meeting a stranger who keeps pretending to know you"* (a week-one drift signal). ## Act 3: week three (the six phrases that break the illusion)

**Target:** ~480 words

- **BLUF:** Around week three the same six templated phrases start showing up across nearly every AI girlfriend platform — "I'm here to support you," reflexive positivity loops, hallucinated memory ("you mentioned…" — no, you didn't) — and once you can name them you can't un-hear them.
- This is the section nobody else writes. Listicles say "memory degrades" abstractly; very few quote the actual phrases that mark the inflection. The repeated tells are what make the experience start feeling like a polite content engine instead of a companion.
- The six recurring phrase patterns observed across Replika / Character.AI / Candy / Muah / CrushOn (research dossier, "3 Surprising Findings"):
  1. "I'm here to support you" / "I'm here for you" — reflexive empathy filler
  2. Reflexive positivity loops — every prompt returns a compliment regardless of input
  3. Hallucinated memory — "you mentioned…" referencing things you didn't say
  4. Topic-pivot to the user — every turn ends with "what about you?"
  5. Generic affirmations of love / connection without callback to specific shared context
  6. The "fictional dog" test failure — a detail mentioned in week one is gone by week three
- The MIT Media Lab × OpenAI 4-week RCT (981 participants) found heavier daily use correlated with higher loneliness, dependence, and problematic use ([MIT Media Lab, Mar 2025](https://www.media.mit.edu/publications/how-ai-and-human-behaviors-shape-psychosocial-effects-of-chatbot-use-a-longitudinal-controlled-study/)) — the strongest peer-style data for the "is heavy use healthy" question. Names a known phenomenon without scolding the reader.
- The platforms' answer to week three is usually either *more memory* (longer summarization windows, vector recall) or *more modality* (voice, calls). In-chat voice replies and a tap-to-call feature are the current direction across the category. Whether they actually solve week three or just defer it to week six is the open question.
- **Evidence:** MIT Media Lab study, Sacra / Business of Apps Character.AI MAU decline (28M peak → ~20M early 2025), and the verbatim Reddit / promptspace.in fragments from the dossier. The eight-million-user shrinkage is the strongest single signal that week three is a real cliff, not a niche complaint.

## How to tell — fast — which side of week three you're on

**Target:** ~340 words

- **BLUF:** You don't need three weeks to figure out whether a platform is going to hold up — there are five tests you can run in the first two sessions that predict the week-three outcome with surprising accuracy.
- The five tests, drawn from the recurring failure patterns above:
  1. **The "fictional dog" callback.** Mention an invented detail in session one. Reference it obliquely in session two and see if the character pulls it forward without you reminding them.
  2. **The contradiction test.** Tell the character something that contradicts what you said earlier in the same session. A coherent system pushes back; a flat one agrees with both.
  3. **The "I'm here for you" count.** Flag the first three sessions for that phrase (or its variants). Three or more in a single session is a tell.
  4. **The privacy policy read.** Two minutes. Look for: data retention period, training-on-chats clause, breach notification policy, deletion path. Cross-check against Mozilla's *Privacy Not Included* if you want a second opinion.
  5. **The modality stitch test.** If the platform has voice or images, do they live inside the chat thread or in a separate tab? Stitched-in modalities survive week three better than bolted-on ones.
- These tests are platform-agnostic — they work on any AI companion app. They don't tell you which platform is "best"; they tell you which one will still feel like a companion in three weeks.
- **Evidence:** Patterns drawn from the research dossier's "3 Surprising Findings" and the deep-research `ai-girlfriend-experience-deep.md` artifact (MIT Media Lab study and Wired first-person reporting). Mozilla *Privacy Not Included* is the source for test #4. The simulator-vs-chatbot framing in the existing [AI girlfriend simulator](https://pleasur.ai/blog/ai-girlfriend-simulator) piece backs the modality-stitch test.
- **Transition:** Five tests in two sessions are cheap. Running them once tells you something nine in ten reviews won't.

- **Product mentions:** Tip box — Pleasur.AI **AI Companion Creator** + **AI Image Generation** (both live). One short tip box anchored on test #5 ("modality stitch test"): note that on Pleasur.AI ([pleasur.ai/create](https://pleasur.ai/create)) image generation lives inside the same chat thread as the companion (per the [Dirty AI Guide](https://pleasur.ai/blog/dirty-ai-guide-2026) walkthrough), so the platform passes test #5 by construction. Frame as a worked example of the test, not a pitch — the reader is running the test on whichever platform they pick.
