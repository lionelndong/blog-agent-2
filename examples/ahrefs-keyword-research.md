# How to Do Keyword Research for SEO (Start to Finish)

## Introduction

Ninety percent of pages on the internet get no organic search traffic from Google. All that effort yields zero visitors—a preventable outcome through keyword research.

Keyword research is the process of discovering valuable search queries that your target customers type into search engines like Google to look for products, services, and information.

Keywords form the foundation of SEO. Publishing content on topics nobody searches for guarantees no traffic from Google, regardless of effort. According to Ahrefs' study of over 1 billion pages, 90.63% receive no organic search traffic.

Keyword research ensures search demand exists for your content topics. When your page ranks well for its target keyword, you'll enjoy consistent streams of highly targeted visitors.

This isn't rocket science—you'll learn most of it in about 20 minutes. However, important caveats and misconceptions require awareness to make better SEO decisions.

## Part 1: Getting started with keyword research

Effective keyword research begins by putting yourself in your customers' shoes. What words and phrases might they use to find solutions to their problems?

Plug these into a keyword research tool like Ahrefs' Keywords Explorer, and you'll discover thousands more relevant keyword ideas beyond your brainstorming.

Success requires two things:

1. Good knowledge of your industry
2. Understanding how keyword research tools work

### Try out free keyword tools

Every keyword research tool asks for a "seed keyword"—a starting point used to generate large lists of keyword ideas.

If you have a product or business, seed keywords come easily. Simply think about what people type into Google to find what you offer.

For example, a coffee business might use seed keywords like:

- coffee
- cappuccino
- french press
- nespresso

Once you have ideas (don't obsess), enter them into free tools like Ahrefs Free Keyword Generator Tool.

For each seed keyword, you'll receive 20 related keywords and 20 related questions—40 keyword ideas immediately. Results show ranking difficulty (easy, medium, or hard) and approximate monthly search volume.

You can repeat this for all seed keywords as needed.

ChatGPT offers another brainstorming avenue. Paste seed keywords with a prompt like "Suggest 10 short keyword ideas for each of the following topics" and receive numerous suggestions.

ChatGPT's downside: it won't provide realistic SEO metrics like keyword difficulty or monthly searches. However, it generates abundant ideas to feed into SEO tools.

Other free keyword research tools exist with limitations:

- **Google Keyword Planner** — Designed for those running Google ads; shows SEO metrics only for active ad campaigns
- **Google Trends** — Shows big, broad topics but lacks specific keyword details
- **Answer the Public** — Uses Google autocomplete data but limits you to 3 daily searches

Once serious about growing search traffic, upgrade to Ahrefs and explore Keywords Explorer. Take a seed keyword and use AI to suggest additional seeds—you'll receive 20 extra keywords to explore.

Search the results and navigate to the Matching terms report. You may find 1.7 million keywords for coffee-related seeds. While overwhelming, you'll learn to narrow this down later.

### See what keywords your website already ranks for

If you have an existing website, find keywords you already rank for. This generates similar keyword ideas or helps improve existing page rankings.

Google Search Console shows search queries your website currently ranks for and receives clicks from. Its limitation: no SEO metrics. If a keyword sends a few monthly clicks, Search Console won't tell you whether it generates 10 or 10,000 monthly searches, or how difficult it would be to improve rankings.

Ahrefs Free solves this. While Google Search Console shows only your top 1,000 keywords, Ahrefs Free displays all of them with Keyword Difficulty scores and monthly search volume.

### See what keywords your competitors rank for

Now that you see your own ranking keywords, wouldn't it be valuable to see your competitors' rankings?

Search Google for a seed keyword and identify front-page competitors. Choose similar businesses (if you sell coffee equipment, they do too—not cafés or Starbucks).

Plug competitor websites into Ahrefs Site Explorer to check which pages drive most traffic and what keywords these pages target.

After repeating this with several competitors, you'll have a substantial relevant keyword list—and you've barely started.

Repeat this process with as many competitors as possible. Ahrefs Site Explorer includes a "Competing Domains" report showing similar websites based on common ranking keywords.

### Seeing a lot of topics you've already covered?

For established websites, you may find you've already covered most competitors' keywords. In this case, use the Content Gap report in Ahrefs Site Explorer.

It identifies keywords where one or more competitors rank, but you don't. Enter competitor websites in the top section, paste your site in the bottom field, and click "Show keywords."

### Study your niche

Everything discussed so far generates almost unlimited keyword ideas—but keeps you "in the box." Finding new uncovered topics requires studying where your target audience congregates.

Visit industry forums, groups, and Q&A sites to study conversations. For instance, a Reddit thread about Aeropress coffee-to-water ratios revealed the search query "aeropress coffee to water ratio"—150 monthly searches. The fact it received 42 Reddit upvotes indicated people genuinely valued this knowledge, and the thread content could serve as a foundation for future writing.

Beyond forums, existing customers provide excellent keyword ideas. When talking with them, notice their language and questions—this may reveal original keyword opportunities.

## Part 2: Types of keywords

Understanding different keyword types becomes important as research progresses. Entering seed keywords into tools like Keywords Explorer reveals two categories of related keywords: Matching terms and Related terms.

### Matching terms

Matching terms are keyword ideas that match the terms in your seed keywords. The Matching terms report offers two modes: "Terms match" and "Phrase match."

"Terms match" returns keyword ideas containing all seed keyword words in any order. For "coffee machine," you'd get results like:

- coffee vending machine
- machine gun kelly coffee shop

"Phrase match" returns keyword ideas with seed keyword words in exact order:

- best coffee machine
- coffee machine with grinder

### Related terms

Related terms are keyword ideas related to seed keywords but don't necessarily contain them. Two modes exist: "Also rank for" and "Also talk about."

"Also rank for" shows search queries where top-ranking pages for your seed keyword also rank.

"Also talk about" shows words and phrases frequently mentioned on top-ranking pages for your seed keywords.

### Keyword clusters

With thousands of keyword ideas accumulated, creating individual pages for each becomes impractical. Keyword clustering simplifies your list.

Compare search results for "whipped coffee" and "whipped coffee recipe"—top-ranking pages nearly match. Google treats "whipped coffee recipe" as a subtopic of "whipped coffee," so ranking for both is possible with a single page.

This grouping process is keyword clustering. Ahrefs created a Parent Topic metric that groups related keywords almost instantly.

Add keywords to Keywords Explorer, go to Clusters by Parent Topic, and see keywords grouped together. You might see 28 keywords grouped in the "whipped coffee recipe" cluster—a single high-quality guide could rank for all of them.

Clustering by Parent Topic saves time through instant processing. Other clustering methods exist, like term clustering, grouping keywords by common words or phrases rather than search result similarity.

### Keyword intents

Keywords have different intent types. Some keywords indicate commercial or transactional intent—people ready to buy:

- best instant coffee
- cuisinart coffee maker
- coffee table with storage

Others have informational intent—people seeking knowledge:

- how much caffeine in coffee
- coffee cake recipe
- is coffee good for you

Sometimes people seek particular websites or locations—navigational intent:

- coffee near me
- stumptown coffee
- drive thru coffee

Keywords can be branded or unbranded: "starbucks coffee" versus "black coffee."

Understanding intent helps determine necessary content type and prioritize whether a keyword justifies targeting.

Informational keywords benefit from educational blog content; commercial keywords need product landing pages enabling purchases.

Check keyword intent by examining search results. "Home coffee roasting" shows mixed intent: roasting machines for sale (commercial) and Reddit discussions (informational).

Keywords Explorer lets you see hundreds of keywords' intents at once and filter by desired intent.

## Part 3: Keyword SEO metrics explained

Access to millions of keyword ideas helps little without knowing which are best. Manually reviewing them all becomes nearly impossible.

The solution: use SEO metrics to narrow options and separate valuable keywords from poor ones before adding them to your list.

Six keyword metrics help with this process.

### Search volume

Search volume indicates average monthly searches for a keyword. For example, "donald trump" receives 3.1 million monthly U.S. searches.

Important search volume facts:

- It's the number of searches, not searchers—someone searching multiple times monthly contributes multiple counts to volume
- It doesn't equal traffic you'll receive at rank #1—even top-ranked pages rarely exceed 30% of search volume in traffic
- It's an annual average—if a keyword gets 120K December searches and zero other months, monthly volume reports as 10K (120K divided by 12)
- It's country-specific—tools display volumes for selected countries, though global search volume options exist

Most keyword research tools include search volume filters for focusing on specific popularity ranges. Main use cases include:

- Filtering out super high-volume keywords—new sites shouldn't target 10K+ monthly searches due to competitiveness
- Focusing on lower-volume keywords—finding uncompetitive, low-volume keywords where small traffic gains are achievable

Long-tail keywords—longer, more specific phrases—receive less attention than they deserve. Almost nobody targets keywords with fewer than 100 monthly searches, yet "zero volume" keywords, while bringing few monthly visitors individually, accumulate meaningfully. Publishing 100 articles targeting such keywords might yield thousands of annual visitors from highly targeted searches.

Overlooking low search volume keywords represents common rookie mistakes. They're as useful as popular counterparts, often more so due to specificity and commercial value.

Search volumes vary slightly between tools because each calculates and updates differently.

Overall, search volume matters tremendously in SEO. Read a dedicated article for deeper understanding.

### Traffic Potential

Two keywords with nearly equal U.S. search volumes should generate similar traffic if targeted, right? Not necessarily.

Compare top-ranking pages for each keyword by copying their URLs into Ahrefs Site Explorer. One might receive 667 organic traffic while another receives 3,100—a fivefold difference for similar search volumes.

Webpages don't rank for single keywords. Top-ranking pages rank for many keywords simultaneously. The page with lower search volume ranks for 55 total keywords; the other ranks for 406 different keywords.

Different people phrase similar searches differently, yet Google understands and ranks the same page for all variations. Research shows average top-ranking pages also rank for approximately 1,000 similar keywords.

Never blindly rely on single keyword search volume when estimating page traffic. Instead, examine top-ranking pages and total search traffic from all keyword variations they rank for.

Ahrefs developed Traffic Potential to address this. It shows how much total search traffic the top-ranking page for your keyword receives.

In Keywords Explorer, Traffic Potential appears beside search volume, saving time on manual lookups. Both metrics are country-specific; use Site Explorer with "All countries" selected for worldwide search traffic.

### Keyword Difficulty

Experienced SEO professionals manually gauge ranking difficulty by examining search results. They consider many factors:

- Search intent
- Content depth, relevance, freshness, authority
- Backlink number and quality
- Domain Rating
- SERP features
- And more

This process varies person-to-person with no consensus on exact importance weights.

Difficulty judgments vary based on query type since Google prioritizes different factors for different searches.

This complexity challenges SEO tool creators trying to distill ranking difficulty into simple two-digit numbers.

After consulting many professional SEOs about signals Keyword Difficulty (KD) should factor in, one thing emerged universally: backlinks matter significantly.

Ahrefs based Keyword Difficulty on unique websites linking to top 10 ranking pages.

On the Keyword Difficulty Scale:

- **Easy**: Estimated 0-10 linking websites needed for top 10 ranking
- **Medium**: Estimated 10-50 linking websites needed
- **Hard**: Estimated 50-100 linking websites needed
- **Super Hard**: Estimated 100+ linking websites needed

KD estimates linking websites needed for top 10 ranking, not #1. Reaching #1 presents entirely different challenges.

Many misuse KD by filtering 0-10 and pursuing only easy keywords. However, avoiding high-KD keywords may be mistaken:

1. **Target high-KD keywords sooner, not later** — High-KD keywords require many backlinks, taking time and resources. Creating and promoting pages early pays off.
2. **View high-KD keywords as link opportunities** — High-KD top-ranking pages having many backlinks signals "link-worthy" topics. Publishing original content might attract substantial links.

The bottom line: KD helps understand ranking requirements and topic "link-worthiness"—it shouldn't deter you. Always manually assess keywords before targeting them; no single score captures Google's ranking complexity perfectly.

### Cost Per Click (CPC)

Cost Per Click (CPC) shows what advertisers pay for search result ad clicks. While primarily for advertisers, it serves as a keyword value proxy.

For example, "project management software" has high $30 CPC because searchers seek products to buy. By contrast, "project management methodologies" carries $6 CPC since it's informational—lower software-selling likelihood, thus lower advertiser value.

Important CPC knowledge: it's much more volatile than search volume. While most keywords maintain consistent monthly search demand, CPC changes minute-to-minute as companies adjust ad displays.

This means SEO tool CPC values are mere snapshots lacking precision. For real-time CPC data, use AdWords.

### Growth

Since search volume represents annual averages, it can mislead about future demand.

Notebook LM shows 73,000 average monthly searches—but this average comes from months with zero volume and months exceeding 400,000 searches. The average underestimates likely near-term search volume.

Similarly, "black friday" averages 282,000 monthly searches from months with 50,000 volume plus one November month with 2.5 million searches.

Keyword search counts change constantly. Real benefits exist for ranking established keywords with low current volume that are already gaining popularity.

Find these trending keywords using Keywords Explorer's "Growth" metric. Sort by Growth to find topics gaining popularity (like "health benefits of mushroom coffee") or declining (like "home coffee roasting").

Google Trends offers free trending search analysis, with extensive guidance available on its use.

### Business Potential

Deciding keyword prioritization and investment allocation is SEO's least straightforward, most individualized part. Many considerations matter:

- Estimated traffic potential
- Ranking difficulty and competitive landscape
- Perfect page creation requirements, or improvements needed
- Business value from ranking for this keyword

That last point proves particularly important. While search volume, traffic potential, ranking difficulty, and intent matter, factor in what ranking delivers to your business.

Ahrefs developed Business Potential—a simple 0-3 scale indicating how easily you can pitch your product while covering a keyword.

| Score | Meaning | Example |
|---|---|---|
| 3 | Product is irreplaceable solution | "how to see competitor's backlinks"—difficult without tools like Ahrefs |
| 2 | Product helps substantially but isn't essential | "SEO tips"—some tips require our product; others don't |
| 1 | Product gets only fleeting mention | "marketing ideas"—SEO is one of many ideas where Ahrefs helps |
| 0 | No way to mention product | "social media marketing"—not something Ahrefs addresses |

If selling Aeropress accessories, "aeropress filters" would score high. Conversely, "stanley thermos," while coffee-related, scores low since your business doesn't address it.

Generally, prioritize topics scoring 2-3 on Business Potential Scale. These most likely bring potential customers to your website—the ultimate keyword research and SEO goal.

When categorizing Ahrefs blog posts, 77% scored high (2-3) on Business Potential, with almost no zero-potential posts.

## Part 4: How to prioritize keywords

Which keywords should you target first? There's no straightforward answer—it depends.

- Are you launching a new website or managing established business?
- Do you work alone or manage large teams?
- Are you responsible for conversions or just generating sales leads?
- How quickly must results appear?

Keyword research means balancing your circumstances with metrics and concepts: traffic potential, keyword difficulty, business potential, and search intent.

Sometimes your job demands maximum traffic as quickly as possible—find high-volume, low-difficulty keywords. Other times you need leads or conversions—business potential becomes most important.

Keyword research isn't about finding "easy to rank" keywords. It's about finding keywords making sense to your business.

Maintain short-, medium-, and long-term ranking goals. Focusing only on short-term prevents ranking for lucrative keywords. Focusing only on medium/long-term means years pass before traffic appears.
