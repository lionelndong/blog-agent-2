# Editorial principles for visual decisions

> The visual taxonomy in `visual-types.md` tells you *what* kinds of visuals exist. This file tells you *how dense* and *what kind* — using the same editorial principles that govern the prose.
>
> Read this BEFORE assigning a `Visual:` type to any section.

## North star: the ahrefs reference

Open one tab to [Ryan Law — Agentic AI vs Generative AI](https://ahrefs.com/blog/agentic-ai-vs-generative-ai/). Count the images: ~10–14 per article. Note the *mix* — diagrams that explain a concept, screenshots that prove a claim, charts that anchor a stat, embedded videos where motion matters, occasional photographs. Note that **none** of them are decorative. Every visual either:

- Explains a concept faster than the next paragraph could (a labeled diagram, a flow chart drawn as illustration).
- Proves a claim with concrete evidence (a screenshot of the actual UI, a chart with sourced data, an external citation).
- Embeds motion the prose cannot replicate (a short demo video, a looping GIF of a multi-step interaction).

That is the bar. We are not counting visuals to hit a number — we are giving every section the visual support it deserves.

## Density target

| Article length | Target visual count | Acceptable range |
|---|---|---|
| < 1,200 words | 5 | 4–7 |
| 1,200–2,000 words | 8 | 6–11 |
| 2,000–3,000 words | 10 | 8–13 |
| > 3,000 words | 12 | 10–15 |

Aim for **type diversity**: at least 3 distinct types per article (e.g. `image` + `screenshot` + `chart`, or `image` + `external` + `video`). A blog with 8 screenshots and nothing else reads narrow even when each screenshot earns its place.

The intro and conclusion can each carry one visual. Long H2s (>400 words) often deserve two — a leading concept visual to anchor the section, and a follow-up that proves a specific claim.

## The core principle (unchanged)

**Every visual earns its place.** A visual must carry information the prose can't, or it gets cut. The change from earlier guidance is *where the burden of proof sits*: the new default for any non-trivial section is "this section deserves a visual; what kind?", not "this section gets no visual unless I can justify one." Most sections in a well-researched, well-outlined article have something concrete to show — a screenshot of the thing being discussed, a diagram of the concept being explained, a chart of the data being cited, an external source being quoted. If you genuinely cannot find one of those, *then* default to `none`.

## Ryan's principles, applied to visuals

### 1. BLUF — Bottom Line Up Front

The opening sentence of every section is the most important sentence. A visual that delays the answer is worse than no visual.

- ✅ Visual *after* the BLUF, supporting evidence the reader is now ready to absorb.
- ❌ Visual at the top of the section as decoration (banner image, "hero" graphic with no explanatory load).
- ❌ Visual that requires reader explanation before they can read the BLUF.

### 2. Show with examples (not abstract claims)

Concrete beats abstract. Bias toward visuals that *show a real thing*:

- ✅ `screenshot` — the brand product doing the exact thing the section discusses.
- ✅ `chart` — real, sourced numbers from the research dossier.
- ✅ `external` — a real Reddit thread, tweet, news headline, or competitor UI being discussed.
- ✅ `image` (concept-illustration) — a labeled diagram explaining a concept the prose introduces (how the memory layer works, how a workflow flows, what the feedback loop looks like). Generated by `openai/gpt-image-2` with a *specific, structured* prompt — not "an AI girlfriend".
- ❌ `image` (lifestyle / mood) — generic "happy user with phone" or abstract "AI brain glow" stock-style imagery. These add zero information.

A good test: **rewrite the section's content as a one-sentence question, then ask "would a labeled diagram, a screenshot, a chart, or a quoted source answer it?"** If yes, you have your visual. If no — and only then — default to `none`.

### 3. Demonstrate; don't sell

When the section mentions a brand product, show it working — don't decorate around it.

- ✅ `screenshot` of the actual feature being used (not the marketing page).
- ✅ `action-shot` when the feature being shown requires interaction — a chat with a response visible, settings with toggles flipped, a wizard mid-flow.
- ❌ `image` of a "happy user" or "abstract concept of the product."
- ❌ Hero shot of a logo. Logos do not earn placement.

### 4. Inverted pyramid (most important info first)

A reader who skims only the intro + first sentence of each H2 should still get the gist. Visuals that *only* make sense after deep reading hurt that.

- ✅ `chart` with a clear title that conveys the takeaway in one glance.
- ✅ `table` placed where the comparison adds skim-able structure.
- ✅ `image` (concept-illustration) with labeled parts, readable at a glance.
- ❌ Complex diagram that requires reading the surrounding paragraphs to understand.

### 5. MECE (no overlap, no gaps)

Sections don't repeat each other. Visuals shouldn't either.

- Don't use the same visual (or near-identical) twice.
- If the same `screenshot` would fit two sections, the sections probably overlap — fix the outline first.
- Don't use both a chart AND a table to show the same data; pick one.
- **Multiple visuals in one section is fine** as long as they cover *different* aspects (e.g. a chart of *what* + a screenshot of *how the brand handles it*).

### 6. Cite everything

If a visual makes a numerical claim, the data behind it has to be sourced.

- ✅ `chart` whose data field references the research dossier or a named source.
- ✅ `image` (concept-illustration) where the *labels* are accurate (a flow diagram of how a feature works must match the actual feature behavior).
- ❌ `chart` with made-up numbers. (`render_chart.py` will not invent data — if data is missing, the chart fails and routes to manual capture.)
- ❌ `image` (concept-illustration) prompts that ask gpt-image-2 to invent statistics or fabricate UI screens that don't exist. Use `screenshot` for real UI; use `image` for *abstract* concepts and *real* flows we describe.

### 7. Reader's time is precious

Captured PNGs at appropriate resolution are fine; embedded videos are heavier and should be reserved for sections where motion is the point.

- ✅ `video` only when a demo genuinely cannot be conveyed as a series of screenshots.
- ✅ `gif` for short, looping interactions where motion is the meaning.
- ❌ Auto-playing video in the intro.

## Decision sequence (apply per H2)

For each H2 section, work through these questions in order. The first "yes" wins.

1. **Does this section walk through a brand product UI at a static URL** (the wanted state is visible on first load, possibly after one age-gate click)?
   → `screenshot` (target = product slug from brand-config; what = the specific UI element)

2. **Does this section need a UI state that only exists after multi-step interaction** — a chat in mid-flow, a wizard past step 1, settings with toggles flipped, a page behind aggressive bot protection where `screenshot` fails?
   → `action-shot` (goal = full natural-language sequence; url = starting URL; what = caption)

3. **Does this section compare N things across M dimensions?**
   → `table` (write inline as markdown; no asset file)

4. **Does this section present quantitative data with trend / proportion / distribution?**
   → `chart` (data must reference the research dossier or be sourced inline)

5. **Does this section reference a third-party artifact** (Reddit thread, tweet, competitor screenshot)?
   → `external` with a `selector` clipping to the element — pipeline auto-captures.

6. **Does this section explain a concept, mental model, workflow, or "how it works" idea** that a labeled diagram would explain faster than the next paragraph?
   → `image` (sub: `concept-illustration` or `diagram`) — Replicate gpt-image-2 with a specific structured prompt.

7. **Does this section explain a multi-step interaction that text struggles with?**
   → `gif` (manual; flagged for editor)

8. **Does this section embed an existing demo video where motion is essential?**
   → `video` (manual; provide embed URL)

9. **Would an aesthetic / scene-setting illustration add specific atmospheric value, SFW?**
   This is the bar most "yes" answers fail. The illustration must convey something the prose can't. If it's purely decorative, the answer is no.
   → `image` (sub: `lifestyle`) only if the answer is genuinely yes.

10. **Otherwise:** `none`. Argue with prose.

### `screenshot` vs `action-shot` — quick test

Ask: **"Could a developer paste a single URL into a fresh browser and see this state immediately?"**

- Yes → `screenshot` (patchright headless, free, fast)
- No (state requires clicks/typing/waiting/login chain) → `action-shot` (handed to `/capture-visuals`, which drives the VPS's always-on Chrome via the Claude in Chrome MCP — also free, just slower because it's a real browser doing real actions)

When in doubt between the two, prefer `screenshot` first — it's faster.

## The `image` type, reframed

Historically `image` was treated as "lifestyle / mood" decoration and most outliners shied away. That was correct *when the only prompt patterns were vibe-y stock-photo descriptions*. With `openai/gpt-image-2` we can produce **labeled diagrams, flow charts drawn as illustration, mental-model maps, before/after concept comparisons** — visuals that meaningfully explain. This is the ahrefs reference: most of their "images" are concept illustrations, not mood shots.

Two sub-categories of `image`:

| Sub | Use | Example prompt |
|---|---|---|
| `concept-illustration` / `diagram` | Explain a concept, mental model, workflow, or relationship the prose introduces. Highest-value `image` use. | `Flow diagram showing how a memory-augmented chat works: user message on left, vector store in the middle (3 boxes labeled "embed", "retrieve", "rerank"), LLM on the right, response loop arrow back to user. Clean editorial illustration style, white background, sans-serif labels.` |
| `lifestyle` / `scene` | Atmospheric / scene-setting where the prose alone leaves the reader without a visual anchor (rare; high bar). | `Modern apartment interior at evening, warm desk lamp, laptop with chat interface visible (no readable text), no people. Photorealistic, editorial.` |

Default `sub` for new `image` placeholders is `concept-illustration` unless a clear scene-setting need exists. Keep prompts **specific, structured, and label-explicit** — vague prompts produce vague art.

## Test for an existing `Visual:` assignment

Before saving the outline, for every section that has a non-`none` Visual, ask:

- **Earns its place?** If the visual is removed, does the reader lose specific, concrete information? If no → change to `none` *or* swap type.
- **First-sentence-readable section?** Would the reader understand the section's BLUF without the visual? (They should — the visual *supports* the BLUF, doesn't *replace* it.) If the section depends on the visual to make sense, the BLUF is wrong.
- **Concrete?** Does it show a real thing (UI, data, source) or a real concept (a diagram with accurate labels) — not a stylized abstraction?
- **MECE?** Does any other section have the same visual? If yes → consolidate or pick a different angle.

And for every section *without* a visual, ask:

- **Could a labeled diagram make this section twice as good?** If yes → add `image` (sub: `concept-illustration`).
- **Is there a screenshot, chart, table, or external source the prose is currently describing in words?** If yes → add the appropriate type.

## Section types that *can* default to `none`

These often work fine without a visual:

- Pure transitional / connective sections
- Argumentative / persuasive sections that turn on rhetoric, not evidence
- Conclusion / recap (one summary visual is fine but not required)
- Very short sections (< 150 words)

Even in these cases, ask whether a small `image` (concept-illustration) would help anchor the argument.

## When in doubt

The article should *feel* like ahrefs. If the published preview shows long stretches of unbroken prose with two screenshots clustered at the bottom, you under-shot. If it shows a visual roughly every 200–300 words, with type variety, you hit the bar.
