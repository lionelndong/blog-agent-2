# Prose Patterns

Specific sentence-level patterns that work for this brand's voice. Use these as building blocks, not formulas.

## Worked example — what right-rhythm prose looks like

The most reliable way to land voice on the first pass is to read this paragraph and pattern your prose against it.

> Most of what people call "AI porn" is one of two things, and the gap between them matters. On one side: consensual generative tools an adult uses to make their own image, voice, or chat. On the other: deepfake tools that target real people without consent. The SERP collapses both into one search; the law treats them very differently. If you're reading this to understand the category, you want the first thing. If a tool can't tell you which side it's on, that's the answer.

Count the moves: 5 sentences in 6 lines. Avg paragraph: ~30 words. One em-dash. Six "you/your" / "people". Direct claim → split → SERP fact → reader-direct framing → closing line that lands. No throat-clearing, no "Furthermore," no "It is important to note." That's the rhythm.

What this paragraph gets right:
- **BLUF in sentence 1.** No setup before the bottom line.
- **Concrete contrast** ("On one side… On the other side…") instead of abstract framing.
- **Cited fact embedded as a clause**, not held back for a separate sentence.
- **Talks to the reader** ("If you're reading this to understand…"), not about the topic.
- **Each sentence advances the argument.** No restatement. No padding.

When a paragraph drifts long, look for the padding. The pattern that produces 60-word paragraphs is the four-clause sentence — `[Topic] is [type] — [list of features] — [comparison] — [implication]`. Cut it into two sentences and the rhythm comes back.

## Opening patterns that work

**Direct claim** — "Keyword cannibalization is when two pages on your site compete for the same query."
**Surprising stat (cited)** — "[According to Ahrefs](https://...), 90.63% of pages get no organic traffic."
**Opinionated** — "Most SEO advice on [topic] is wrong, and here's why."
**Problem-naming (you-voice)** — "You've published a dozen articles on the same topic and watched your rankings drop. That's keyword cannibalization."
**Contrarian** — "Forget what you've read about backlink counts."

## Transitions that work

- "Here's the thing." (use sparingly, max once per article)
- "But there's a catch."
- "That's the theory. Here's what actually happens."
- "The result?"
- "More on this in a moment."

## Transitions to avoid

- "Furthermore," "Moreover," "Additionally"
- "It is important to note that"
- "With that said"
- "Now, let's move on to"
- "In the next section, we'll discuss"

## Showing instead of telling

**Don't say:** "Ahrefs is a powerful keyword research tool with many features."
**Do say:** "Plug a domain into Ahrefs' Site Explorer and you'll see every keyword it ranks for, with traffic estimates and difficulty scores."

**Don't say:** "It's important to track your competitors."
**Do say:** "Open Site Explorer, paste your competitor's domain, and look at their Top Pages report. The pages driving their traffic are the same ones you should be writing better versions of."

## Handling stats

- **Always cite.** No bare numbers. `[link the source].`
- **Don't pad.** "Studies show that 67% of marketers..." — drop "Studies show that"; the link does the work. Just say "67% of marketers..."
- **Round when sensible.** "Almost 9 out of 10 pages get no traffic" reads better than "89.4% of pages get no traffic" in the body. Save the precise number for the source link.

## Handling product mentions

The pattern that works:

1. State a problem the reader has
2. Describe the manual way to solve it
3. Mention the product as the faster way
4. Show what the product does (specific feature/output)

Example:
> Finding cannibalized pages by hand means running a `site:yourdomain.com "your keyword"` search in Google and squinting at the results. Or — open ProductA's Cannibalization Report. It surfaces every page on your site that competes with another page for the same query, sorted by impact.

What doesn't work:
> ProductA is the best tool for finding keyword cannibalization. It has a Cannibalization Report and many other powerful features that help SEO professionals do their work.

## List patterns

**Use bullet lists when:** items are roughly parallel, order doesn't strictly matter, and you have 3+ items.

**Use numbered lists when:** order matters (steps in a process) or you'll reference items later by number.

**Don't use lists when:** there are only 2 items, or the items have different weights/levels of importance — write prose.

**Don't bullet-spam.** A 200-word section with three bullet lists is a sign you stopped writing. Weave at least some claims into prose.

## Tables

Use a table when you're comparing 3+ things on 2+ dimensions. A 2x2 table is usually better as prose. A 5x5 table is your friend.

Always include a header row. Put the most important column second (people skim left-most column then jump to the right).

## Examples > theory

When the section gets abstract, anchor it with an example. The reader doesn't need a definition of "topical authority" if you show two sites and explain why one has it.

## Direct address

Use "you" when describing what the reader does. Use "we" sparingly — only for shared experience or when introducing data the brand collected.

## Length signals

- 800–1500 words: short explainer, single concept
- 1500–3000 words: full how-to or guide on one tactic
- 3000–6000 words: definitive guide on a parent topic
- Over 6000: you're probably trying to cover too much; check MECE
